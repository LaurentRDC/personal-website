Electrodynamics primer

Electromagnetic waves, such as radar waves and light, are waves composed of two vector fields known as the electric field and magnetic field. This means that everywhere in the universe, there are two vectors ùêÑ(ùê´,t)\textbf{E}(\textbf{r}, t) and ùêÅ(ùê´,t)\textbf{B}(\textbf{r}, t) whose magnitudes and directions must be understood.

All this to say: understanding the propagation of an electromagnetic wave requires to know 6 time-varying quantities: the electric field components ùêÑ(ùê´,t)=(Ex(t),Ey(t),Ez(t))\textbf{E}(\textbf{r}, t) = (E_x(t), E_y(t), E_z(t)) and the magnetic field components ùêÅ(ùê´,t)=(Bx(t),By(t),Bz(t))\textbf{B}(\textbf{r},t) = (B_x(t), B_y(t), B_z(t)), over the space- and time-domain of our simulation. If you know these 6 quantities, you know everything.

Let‚Äôs rip the bandaid off. Electromagnetic waves are described by a set of partial differential equations known as Maxwell‚Äôs equations. When I said this blog post will be from first principles, the first principles are these equations1.

‚àá‚ãÖùêÉ(ùê´,t)=œÅf(ùê´,t)Gauss‚Äô law‚àá‚ãÖùêÅ(ùê´,t)=0Gauss‚Äô law of magnetism‚àá√óùêÑ(ùê´,t)=‚àí‚àÇùêÅ(ùê´,t)‚àÇtMaxwell-Faraday‚Äôs law‚àá√óùêá(ùê´,t)=ùêâf(ùê´,t)+‚àÇùêÉ(ùê´,t)‚àÇtAmp√®re-Maxwell‚Äôs law \begin{align} \nabla \cdot \textbf{D}(\textbf{r}, t) &= \rho_f(\textbf{r}, t) &&& \text{Gauss' law}\\ \nabla \cdot \textbf{B}(\textbf{r}, t) &= 0 &&& \text{Gauss' law of magnetism} \\ \nabla \times \textbf{E}(\textbf{r}, t) &= -\frac{\partial \textbf{B}(\textbf{r}, t) }{\partial t} &&& \text{Maxwell-Faraday's law}\\ \nabla \times \textbf{H}(\textbf{r}, t) &= \textbf{J}_f(\textbf{r}, t) + \frac{\partial \textbf{D}(\textbf{r}, t) }{\partial t} &&& \text{Amp\`ere-Maxwell's law} \end{align}

where ùêá\textbf{H} is the auxiliary magnetic field. We use a formulation using the auxiliary field ùêá\textbf{H} rather than the magnetic field ùêÅ\textbf{B} such that we only need to worry about the free current density ùêâf\textbf{J}_f, rather than the total current density.

There are also the constitutive equations:

ùêÉ(ùê´,t)=œµ(ùêÑ(ùê´,t),ùê´,t)ùêÑ(ùê´,t)ùêâf(ùê´,t)=œÉ(ùêÑ(ùê´,t),ùê´,t)ùêÑ(ùê´,t)ùêÅ(ùê´,t)=Œº(ùêá(ùê´,t),ùê´,t)ùêá(ùê´,t) \begin{align} \textbf{D}(\textbf{r}, t) &= \epsilon (\textbf{E}(\textbf{r}, t), \textbf{r}, t) \textbf{E}(\textbf{r}, t) \\ \textbf{J}_f(\textbf{r}, t) &= \sigma (\textbf{E}(\textbf{r}, t), \textbf{r}, t) \textbf{E}(\textbf{r}, t) \\ \textbf{B}(\textbf{r}, t) &= \mu (\textbf{H}(\textbf{r}, t), \textbf{r}, t) \textbf{H}(\textbf{r}, t) \\ \end{align}

where œµ\epsilon, œÉ\sigma, and Œº\mu are the permittivity, conductivity, and permeability, respectively. These quantities are properties of materials, and as such are not considered unknown. Note that we do not assume any frequency-dependent response of materials; we consider time-dependence of material properties in the most general manner.

Recall that our goal is to solve these equations over space (ùê´\textbf{r}) and time (tt) to get values of the electric field ùêÑ(ùê´,t)\textbf{E}(\textbf{r}, t) and auxiliary magnetic field ùêá(ùê´,t)\textbf{H}(\textbf{r}, t). To this end, Gauss‚Äôs laws of electric and magnetic fluxes won‚Äôt be directly useful to us today (although they could be used to ensure the integrity of the computation). We will instead solve Faraday‚Äôs and Amp`ere‚Äôs laws iteratively, until some condition is met.

We combine the equations above to get two coupled equations for ùêÑ\textbf{E} and ùêá\textbf{H}:

‚àÇùêÑ‚àÇt=1œµ(‚àá√óùêá)‚àÇùêá‚àÇt=‚àí1Œº(‚àá√óùêÑ) \begin{align} \frac{\partial \textbf{E}}{\partial{t}} &= \frac{1}{\epsilon} \left( \nabla \times \textbf{H} \right) \\ \frac{\partial \textbf{H}}{\partial{t}} &= -\frac{1}{\mu} \left( \nabla \times \textbf{E} \right) \\ \end{align}

subject to initial and boundary conditions.

These equations can be solved in a multitude of ways, usually based on the structure of œµ\epsilon, œÉ\sigma, and Œº\mu, as well as boundary and initial conditions. For example, for a cavity composed of two planar mirrors with an initial standing wave can be solved analytically.

In general, one should solve Maxwell‚Äôs equations by exploiting structures and symmetries as much as possible. This leads to more precise solutions that can be reached much quicker. There are instances, however, where many standard tools break down, and a more general approach must be taken. One such instance involves so-called broadband sources, the name given to pulses of light2.

In this particular post, however, I want to take a much more general approach to demonstrate a computational technique, Yee‚Äôs method3, which is broadly applicable.

Numerical solution using Yee‚Äôs method

Time to numerically solve these equations by following , sometimes called the finite-difference time-domain method. Consider a small simulation time step of value Œît\Delta t. We consider the approximation to the derivative in the so-called central difference scheme. Consider a general function FF of some one-dimensional variable zz:

‚àÇùêÖ(z)‚àÇz=ùêÖ(z+Œîz)‚àíùêÖ(z‚àíŒîz)2Œîz+ùí™(Œîz2) \frac{\partial \textbf{F}(z)}{\partial z} = \frac{ \textbf{F}(z + \Delta z) - \textbf{F}(z - \Delta z)}{2 \Delta z} + \mathcal{O}(\Delta z^2)

where ùí™(Œîz2)\mathcal{O}(\Delta z^2) is a collection of terms that are proportional to Œîz2\Delta z^2. Since Œîz\Delta z will be chosen to be suitably small, we will be ignore terms proportional to Œîz2\Delta z^2.

Discretizing the time derivative using the central difference scheme leads to the two update equations, one for the auxiliary magnetic field:

ùêá(ùê´,t+Œ¥t2)=ùêá(ùê´,t‚àíŒ¥t2)‚àíŒ¥tŒº(ùê´,t)(‚àá√óùêÑ(ùê´,t)) \textbf{H}(\textbf{r}, t + \tfrac{\delta t}{2}) = \textbf{H}(\textbf{r}, t - \tfrac{\delta t}{2}) - \frac{\delta t}{\mu(\textbf{r}, t)} \left( \nabla \times \textbf{E}(\textbf{r}, t) \right)

and one for the electric field:

ùêÑ(ùê´,t+Œ¥t)=ùêÑ(ùê´,t)+Œ¥tœµ(ùê´,t)(‚àá√óùêá(ùê´,t+Œ¥t2)) \textbf{E}(\textbf{r}, t + \delta t) = \textbf{E}(\textbf{r}, t) + \frac{\delta t}{\epsilon(\textbf{r}, t)} \left( \nabla \times \textbf{H}(\textbf{r}, t + \tfrac{\delta t}{2}) \right)

where Œ¥t‚â°2Œît\delta t \equiv 2 \Delta t is conventionally used. You will notice the leapfrog time quantization; the electric field update is offset by Œ¥t/2\delta t/2 from the auxiliary magnetic field update. This scheme, and the space quantization scheme that will be presented below, are used to ensure that Gauss‚Äô laws of electric and magnetic fluxes are respected4.

We also quantize space, such that the fields are defined on discrete positions ùê´ijk=iŒîùê±+jŒîùê≤+kŒîùê≥\textbf{r}_{ijk} = i \Delta ~ \textbf{x} + j \Delta ~ \textbf{y} + k \Delta ~ \textbf{z}, where ùê±\textbf{x}, ùê≤\textbf{y}, and ùê≥\textbf{z} are unit vectors in the three dimensions, and ii, jj, kk are integer indices that span the size of the space we will investigate. We will switch to a different notation for discretized quantities:

ùêÖ(ùê´=iŒîùê±+jŒîùê≤+kŒîùê≥,t=nŒ¥t)‚â°ùêÖ[i,j,k,n] \textbf{F}(\textbf{r} = i \Delta ~ \textbf{x} + j \Delta ~ \textbf{y} + k \Delta ~ \textbf{z}, t = n \delta t) \equiv \textbf{F}[i,j,k,n]

Discretizing the time derivative was rather straightforward. Now, it is time to discretize terms of the form

‚àá√óùêÄ(ùê´) \nabla \times \textbf{A}(\textbf{r})

where ùêÄ\textbf{A} is some vector field defined in three rectilinear dimensions. Recall the non-discretized definition:

where AiA_i is the component of ùêÄ\textbf{A} in the ùê¢\textbf{i}-direction. Again using the central difference scheme, we discretize the partial derivatives using a common step size across all three dimensions, Œî\Delta:
